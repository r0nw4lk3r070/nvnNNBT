# 00 — History of European Digital Legislation
### LocAI Library · Chronological Overview
_Version 0.1 · March 2026_

---

## Why this document exists

Every law in our library exists for a reason. Each one is a response to something — a technological development, a political incident, a court ruling that changed everything. If you know the history, you understand why the laws are the way they are. And if you know why they are the way they are, you can explain them to others.

---

## The Timeline

### 1948 — The Foundation: Universal Declaration of Human Rights

Article 12:
> "No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence."

This is the foundation. Everything Europe subsequently builds in digital legislation is an attempt to uphold this right in an increasingly digital world.

Not coincidentally: Europe has a collective memory of what happens when governments have unrestricted access to the communications and lives of citizens. Germany (Stasi), Eastern Europe (KGB networks), the Second World War. That memory is embedded in the DNA of European regulation in a way that differs fundamentally from the US or China.

---

### 1980 — OECD Privacy Protection Guidelines

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development publishes the first international guidelines for the protection of personal data. Non-binding, but they introduce for the first time the concepts that would recur in all subsequent European legislation:

- **Purpose limitation** — data may only be used for the purpose for which it was collected
- **Data minimisation** — collect only what is necessary
- **Quality** — data must be accurate and up to date
- **Security** — appropriate measures against loss or misuse
- **Transparency** — data subjects must know what is being done with their data

These principles remain the core of the GDPR 45 years later.

---

### 1981 — Council of Europe Convention 108

The first binding international instrument for data protection. Signed by 55 countries, now modernised as "Convention 108+".

Key point: personal data may not be automatically processed if that poses a threat to the fundamental rights of the data subject.

---

### 1995 — EU Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC)

The first European legislation specifically addressing personal data. A directive — not a regulation. That means: the EU sets the framework, but each member state implements it in national law.

Result: 15 different national laws, 15 different interpretations, 15 different enforcement levels. A company operating in multiple countries had to prove compliance 15 times over.

What it introduced: right of access, right of rectification, prohibition on transfer to countries without an adequate level of protection, registration of processing operations with national supervisory authorities.

What it missed: the internet barely existed. E-commerce was in its infancy. The directive was written for a world of paper files and mainframes.

---

### 2000 — Safe Harbor

Europe and the US reach an agreement: American companies that voluntarily adhere to certain privacy principles may receive European personal data. The US receives an adequacy decision.

In practice: little oversight, little enforcement, many loopholes.

---

### 2001 — September 11 and the Turning Point

Following the attacks in New York, the US fundamentally changed its approach to surveillance. The Patriot Act grants American intelligence agencies far-reaching powers — including access to data of foreign citizens on American servers.

Europe takes note. But takes no structural action yet.

---

### 2002 — ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC)

The "cookie law." Requires consent for placing cookies. In practice: years of cookie banners that no one reads and everyone dismisses. The spirit of the law is not achieved.

A modernisation (the ePrivacy Regulation) has been under negotiation for years. Still not adopted in 2026.

---

### 2013 — Snowden and the World Changes

Edward Snowden leaks NSA documents. The world discovers:

PRISM: the NSA has direct access to servers of Google, Facebook, Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Skype, YouTube and AOL.

XKeyscore: a system that can analyse virtually all internet communications of virtually everyone.

Upstream collection: the NSA taps directly into undersea cables.

For Europe, this is a shock. Not only politically — legally. Because the Safe Harbor agreement presupposes that American companies protect European data. PRISM proves this is an illusion.

Max Schrems, an Austrian lawyer, files a complaint with the Irish supervisory authority against Facebook. The beginning of a legal saga that forever changes the world of data transfers.

---

### 2016 — GDPR Adopted (in force 2018)

After four years of negotiations, the EU publishes the General Data Protection Regulation. Now a regulation — directly binding in all member states. One law for the entire EU.

The motivation is explicit: the 1995 directive is not equipped for the digital reality of 2016. Social media, smartphones, big data, machine learning — none of this existed when the directive was written.

What is new compared to 1995: fines up to €20 million or 4% of global turnover, privacy by design and privacy by default as mandatory principles, right to erasure, right to data portability, mandatory breach notification within 72 hours, mandatory processing agreement, explicit consent required.

What remains the same: the basic principles of 1980 — purpose limitation, data minimisation, transparency — remain unchanged at the core.

---

### 2016 — NIS1 Directive (in force 2018)

While the GDPR concerns personal data, NIS1 concerns the security of networks and information systems at critical infrastructure. Energy, transport, healthcare, digital services.

First European cybersecurity legislation. Again a directive — again national implementation differences.

---

### 2018 — CLOUD Act (US)

Clarifying Lawful Overseas Use of Data Act. Requires American companies to hand over stored data to American authorities, regardless of where that data is physically located.

A Belgian patient record on an American cloud server in Frankfurt falls under the CLOUD Act. This is not an interpretation — this is what the law says.

Europe does not respond immediately with legislation, but the awareness grows: legal sovereignty requires technical sovereignty.

---

### 2020 — Schrems II (CJEU, 16 July)

The Court of Justice of the EU invalidates the EU-US Privacy Shield.

Reason: American surveillance legislation (FISA Section 702, Executive Order 12333) does not offer European citizens protection that is "essentially equivalent" to the protection under EU law.

What this means: every European organisation that transfers personal data to American servers — including via cloud services from Google, Microsoft, Amazon — operates in a legal grey area.

In practice: most European organisations largely ignore this. Enforcement remains limited. But the legal risk is real and documented.

The political impact: Schrems II accelerates the debate on European digital sovereignty. If American legislation can override European privacy rights, there is only one structural solution: keep data and processing within the EU.

---

### 2022 — NIS2 Directive Adopted (in force October 2024)

NIS1 proved insufficient. Too few sectors, too many national differences, too low penalties.

NIS2 expands to 18 sectors, increases penalties, introduces personal liability for directors, and requires security of the entire supply chain.

---

### 2022 — DORA Adopted (in force January 2025)

Specific to the financial sector. Banks and insurers are becoming increasingly dependent on ICT third parties. DORA requires them to map, test and manage these dependencies.

---

### 2023 — EU-US Data Privacy Framework

A new attempt after Schrems II. The US introduces a Presidential Executive Order. The EU declares the US adequate again.

Max Schrems immediately announces he will challenge this agreement too. The legal uncertainty is structural — no agreement seems durable as long as FISA Section 702 exists.

---

### 2023 — Data Act Adopted (in force September 2025)

Focuses on data generated by connected devices and cloud services. Prohibits switching costs from cloud providers from January 2027. Directly relevant for organisations wishing to migrate to local AI.

---

### 2024 — AI Act Adopted (deadline high-risk systems: expected 2 December 2027)

The world's first binding AI law. Builds explicitly on GDPR.

Recital 1:
> "Artificial intelligence should be developed and deployed in a way that safeguards democracy, the rule of law and fundamental rights."

The AI Act did not come out of nowhere. It is the logical next step in a journey that began in 1948.

---

### Coming: ePrivacy Regulation (date unknown)

Under negotiation for years. Replaces the 2002 cookie directive. Once adopted: part of the library.

---

## The Common Thread

Reading the timeline, you see one constant: Europe responds to technological developments that threaten citizens' privacy, and builds ever-stronger legal protection.

1948 — the right to privacy as a human right.
1980 — first principles for data processing.
1995 — first binding EU legislation, but without real teeth.
2013 — Snowden proves that the American cloud is not a safe place for European data.
2016 — GDPR, now with real teeth. One law for the entire EU.
2018 — CLOUD Act: America confirms that their companies can be forced to hand over data on European servers too.
2020 — Schrems II: the Court draws the conclusion many had avoided — the legal basis for data transfers to the US is flawed.
2024 — AI Act: intelligence itself becomes regulated.
