# 02 — NIS2
### LocAI Library · Deep Dive
_Directive (EU) 2022/2555 · In force 17 October 2024_
_Version 0.1 · March 2026_

---

## What this document is

NIS2 is the second version of the Network and Information Security Directive. It replaces NIS1 (2016) and dramatically expands the scope. This document follows the same structure as the GDPR document: literal text, translation, implication for local AI versus cloud AI.

---

## The core in one sentence

NIS2 obliges organisations in 18 sectors to take cybersecurity seriously — and makes directors personally liable if they do not.

---

## Part 1 — Why NIS2 exists

NIS1 was insufficient. Three fundamental problems:

**Too few sectors.** NIS1 only covered "operators of essential services" and "digital service providers." Many critical organisations fell outside it.

**Too many national differences.** NIS1 was a directive — each country implemented it differently. A cybersecurity incident affecting multiple countries was handled differently by each.

**Too little bite.** Fines were low, enforcement was reactive, and directors felt no personal pressure.

NIS2 addresses this. More sectors, more uniformity, higher fines, personal liability.

---

## Part 2 — Who does NIS2 apply to?

### The literal text

NIS2 distinguishes between "essential entities" and "important entities." The classification determines the intensity of supervision, not the obligations themselves.

**Essential entities (Annex I):**
- Energy (electricity, oil, gas, heat, hydrogen)
- Transport (air, rail, water, road)
- Banking
- Financial market infrastructure
- Healthcare
- Drinking water
- Wastewater
- Digital infrastructure (internet exchange points, DNS, TLD registries, cloud providers, data centres, CDN, trust services, electronic communications)
- ICT service providers (managed services, managed security services)
- Government services
- Space

**Important entities (Annex II):**
- Postal and courier services
- Waste management
- Manufacture of chemical substances
- Production, manufacturing and distribution of food
- Manufacturing (medical devices, computers, electronics, machinery, motor vehicles, other transport equipment)
- Digital providers (online marketplaces, search engines, social networks)
- Research organisations

### The translation

If your organisation is active in one of these sectors and meets the thresholds (see below), you fall under NIS2.

**The thresholds:**
- Medium-sized enterprise: 50-249 employees or €10-50 million turnover
- Large enterprise: 250+ employees or €50+ million turnover

Small organisations (fewer than 50 employees and less than €10 million turnover) are in principle exempt — unless they play a critical role in their sector.

**Exceptions that still affect small organisations:**
- Providers of electronic communications networks
- Trust service providers
- DNS resolution services
- Certain public authorities

### The implication for our target audience

An accountancy firm of 8 people probably does not fall directly under NIS2. But:

1. Their clients in the financial sector do.
2. NIS2 requires essential and important entities to also manage the security of their supply chain — including their accountant.
3. The pressure therefore trickles down to smaller suppliers.

A GP practice of 4 doctors: healthcare is in Annex I. The thresholds determine whether they fall directly under NIS2. But here too: the chain requirement applies.

## Part 3 — The core obligations

### The literal text (Art. 21)

> "Member States shall ensure that essential and important entities take appropriate and proportionate technical, operational and organisational measures to manage the risks posed to the security of network and information systems which those entities use for their operations or for the provision of their services, and to prevent or minimise the impact of incidents on recipients of their services and on other services."

Those measures shall include at least:

> "a) policies on risk analysis and information system security;
> b) incident handling;
> c) business continuity, such as backup management and disaster recovery, and crisis management;
> d) supply chain security, including security-related aspects concerning the relationships between each entity and its direct suppliers or service providers;
> e) security in network and information systems acquisition, development and maintenance, including vulnerability handling and disclosure;
> f) policies and procedures to assess the effectiveness of cybersecurity risk-management measures;
> g) basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training;
> h) policies and procedures regarding the use of cryptography and, where appropriate, encryption;
> i) human resources security, access control policies and asset management;
> j) the use of multi-factor authentication or continuous authentication solutions, secured voice, video and text communications and secured emergency communication systems within the entity, where appropriate."

### The translation

Ten mandatory areas of measures. In plain language:

**a) Risk analysis:** know what your digital risks are. Document them. Set policy.

**b) Incident handling:** know what you do when something goes wrong. Who calls whom? What gets shut down? Who informs the regulator?

**c) Business continuity:** ensure you can continue operating after an incident. Backups are not optional. They must also be tested.

**d) Supply chain security:** this is the article that flows through to smaller suppliers. If you supply an essential entity — as an accountant, software supplier, or facilities provider — that entity has an obligation to assess your security practices.

**e) System security:** how do you procure software? How do you test for vulnerabilities? How do you respond when a supplier reports a security flaw?

**f) Effectiveness assessment:** measure whether your measures work. Not just set and forget.

**g) Cyber hygiene and training:** employees know how to recognise phishing. Updates are installed. Password policies exist and are enforced.

**h) Encryption:** sensitive data is encrypted — both in transit and at rest.

**i) Access management:** who has access to what? Multi-factor authentication where possible.

**j) MFA and secure communications:** two-factor authentication is no longer optional — it is an obligation.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

**Supply chain security (d):**
A cloud AI service is a supplier. If you fall under NIS2, you must assess and document the cybersecurity practices of that supplier. For large cloud providers this is a mountain of paperwork. With local AI: the "supplier" is yourself.

**Encryption (h):**
With cloud AI, data travels over the internet to external servers. Encryption in transit is standard, but encryption at rest is provider-dependent. With local AI: you manage the encryption of your own storage.

**Business continuity (c):**
Cloud AI depends on an internet connection and the availability of the provider. If the provider is down, or if your internet connection fails, your AI functionality stops. Local AI works offline.

## Part 4 — Notification obligation (Art. 23)

### The literal text

> "Member States shall ensure that essential and important entities notify the competent authority or the CSIRT without undue delay of any incident having a significant impact on the provision of their services."

With specific timeframes:

> "a) without undue delay and in any event within 24 hours after having become aware of the significant incident, an early warning [...];
> b) without undue delay and in any event within 72 hours after having become aware of the significant incident, an incident notification [...];
> c) upon request of the competent authority or the CSIRT, an intermediate report [...];
> d) a final report at the latest one month after the submission of the incident notification referred to in point b)."

### The translation

If there is a significant incident — cyberattack, data breach, critical system outage — you have:

- 24 hours for an early warning
- 72 hours for a full incident notification
- 1 month for a final report

"Significant" means: serious operational disruption, considerable financial loss, or impact on other organisations.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

In the event of an incident involving a cloud AI service, you depend on the provider for information about what exactly happened. That information may come slowly, be incomplete, or be classified by the provider as commercially sensitive.

You must notify within 24 hours. If the provider only informs you after 48 hours, you already have a problem.

With local AI: the incident is in your own environment. You have the logs. You know what happened. You can act and report immediately.

## Part 5 — Personal liability (Art. 20)

### The literal text

> "Member States shall ensure that the management bodies of essential and important entities approve the cybersecurity risk-management measures taken by those entities pursuant to Article 21, oversee its implementation and can be held liable for infringements by entities of that Article."

And:

> "Member States shall ensure that members of the management bodies of essential and important entities are required to follow training, and shall encourage essential and important entities to offer similar training to their employees on a regular basis."

### The translation

This is the article that keeps directors awake at night.

It is not the IT department that is responsible for cybersecurity. The board of directors, the managing director, the CEO — they are personally liable if the organisation fails to comply with the NIS2 obligations.

"Can be held liable" means in practice: a temporary ban on holding management positions if the organisation has a significant incident attributable to negligence.

Directors must also be trained. Not knowing is no longer an excuse.

### The implication for our target audience

This is the argument that gets a director to the table instead of the IT employee.

"Are you aware that as a director you can be held personally liable for cybersecurity incidents resulting from negligence in taking the required measures?"

That is a different conversation than "have you updated your firewall."

## Part 6 — Fines (Art. 34)

### The literal text

For essential entities:
> "administrative fines of a maximum of EUR 10 000 000 or of a maximum of 2% of the total worldwide annual turnover [...], whichever is higher."

For important entities:
> "administrative fines of a maximum of EUR 7 000 000 or of a maximum of 1,4% of the total worldwide annual turnover [...], whichever is higher."

### The translation

Essential entities (hospitals, energy companies, banks): up to €10 million or 2% of turnover.

Important entities (manufacturing companies, digital providers): up to €7 million or 1.4% of turnover.

Lower than GDPR, but still significant. And combined with personal liability of directors, the pressure is considerable.

## Part 7 — NIS2 and the supply chain

This is the most underestimated aspect of NIS2 for SMEs.

### The literal text (Art. 21(2)(d), further elaborated in Art. 22)

> "Member States shall ensure that essential and important entities take into account the vulnerabilities specific to each direct supplier and service provider and the overall quality of products and cybersecurity practices of their suppliers and service providers."

### The translation

If you supply an organisation that falls under NIS2 — as an accountant, software supplier, or facilities provider — that organisation has an obligation to assess your cybersecurity practices.

In practice: you receive questionnaires. You must be able to demonstrate that you too take security seriously. Otherwise you risk being removed from the supplier list.

### The implication for SME suppliers

An accountant who works for a hospital. An IT supplier who provides software to an energy company. A legal adviser who reviews contracts for a bank.

All suppliers of essential or important entities. All subject to the NIS2 supply chain requirements — even if they are themselves too small to fall directly under the law.

This is the silent ripple effect of NIS2 into the entire SME landscape.

## Part 8 — NIS2 and AI

NIS2 does not mention AI explicitly. But the combination is unavoidable.

**If you use AI as part of your service delivery:**
The AI systems you deploy are part of your "network and information systems." They fall under the security obligation of Art. 21. You must conduct risk analysis, manage access, and be able to demonstrate that the system is secured.

**If you use cloud AI:**
The cloud provider is a supplier. Art. 21(d) obliges you to assess the security practices of that supplier. For a small organisation using ChatGPT: that means in theory you must be able to document and assess OpenAI's security practices.

**If you use local AI:**
The system is on your own infrastructure. You manage the security. There is no external supplier to assess for this system. The compliance burden is significantly lower.

## Part 9 — NIS2 and GDPR combined

The two laws overlap and reinforce each other.

| | NIS2 | GDPR |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Security of systems and networks | Protection of personal data |
| **Incident notification** | 24h warning, 72h notification | 72h notification for data breach |
| **Director liability** | Explicit (Art. 20) | Implicit via accountability obligation |
| **Supply chain** | Explicit requirement (Art. 21d) | Via data processing agreement (Art. 28) |
| **Fines** | Up to €10M or 2% turnover | Up to €20M or 4% turnover |

A data breach at a healthcare institution that also falls under NIS2 triggers both notification obligations simultaneously. Two regulators, two reports, two deadlines.

## Glossary

**Essential entity** — organisations in the most critical sectors (energy, transport, healthcare, water, digital infrastructure, government). Heaviest supervision.

**Important entity** — organisations in sectors that are less critical but still significant (post, waste, chemicals, food, manufacturing, digital providers). Lighter supervision but same obligations.

**CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team)** — national teams that handle and coordinate cyber incidents. In Belgium: CCB (Centre for Cyber Security Belgium). In the Netherlands: NCSC (National Cyber Security Centre).

**Incident** — an event that compromises the availability, authenticity, integrity or confidentiality of stored, transmitted or processed data or of services.

**Significant incident** — an incident causing considerable operational disruption or financial loss, or having an impact on other organisations or persons.

**Supply chain** — the chain of suppliers and service providers contributing to the operation of an organisation. NIS2 requires essential and important entities to also manage the security of this chain.

**Multi-factor authentication (MFA)** — a login method requiring the user to provide two or more pieces of identity evidence. Password plus SMS code, or password plus authenticator app.

**Cyber hygiene** — the basic practices that organisations and individuals must apply to keep digital systems safe. Installing updates, using strong passwords, recognising phishing, making backups.

**CCB (Centre for Cyber Security Belgium)** — the Belgian national cybersecurity authority. Responsible for NIS2 supervision in Belgium. Website: ccb.belgium.be

## Checklist for a client conversation

- [ ] Does your organisation fall under NIS2? (sector + size)
- [ ] Do you supply organisations that fall under NIS2?
- [ ] Do you have a documented risk analysis for your digital systems?
- [ ] Do you have an incident response plan? Does everyone know what to do in the event of a cyber incident?
- [ ] Do you have backups? Are they tested?
- [ ] Do you have multi-factor authentication enabled on critical systems?
- [ ] Have you assessed the cybersecurity practices of your cloud suppliers?
- [ ] Are your directors aware of their personal liability?
- [ ] Have you trained employees in cyber hygiene?
- [ ] Do you have encryption enabled for sensitive data?
