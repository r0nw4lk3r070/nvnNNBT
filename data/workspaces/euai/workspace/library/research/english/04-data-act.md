# 04 — Data Act
### LocAI Library · Deep Dive
_Regulation (EU) 2023/2854 · In force 12 September 2025_
_Version 0.1 · March 2026_

---

## What this document is

The Data Act is the most recent major European data law. It is less well known than the GDPR or AI Act, but it has direct consequences for organisations wishing to migrate from cloud to local solutions. This document follows the same structure: literal text, translation, implication.

---

## The core in one sentence

The Data Act gives users the right to control, request and transfer their data — and prohibits cloud providers from preventing or impeding this.

---

## Part 1 — Why the Data Act exists

Three problems the Data Act aims to solve.

**Problem 1 — Data generated by devices stays with the manufacturer.**
You buy a smart thermostat. It generates valuable data about your energy consumption. But that data automatically goes to the manufacturer — not to you. The Data Act gives you as the user the right to that data.

**Problem 2 — Switching cloud providers is artificially difficult.**
A company wishing to migrate from AWS to Azure, or from cloud to local infrastructure, encounters technical barriers and financial thresholds. Providers have an interest in keeping you. The Data Act prohibits switching obstacles.

**Problem 3 — Government authorities cannot request data in emergencies.**
During disasters or public emergencies, government authorities sometimes need data held by private companies. The Data Act creates a legal framework for such exceptional access.

---

## Part 2 — Who does the Data Act apply to?

The Data Act has a broad scope but imposes different obligations on different parties.

**Manufacturers of connected products and providers of related services:**
Everything that generates data and is connected to the internet. Smart devices, IoT sensors, industrial machinery, medical equipment, vehicles.

**Providers of data processing services:**
Cloud providers, data centres, SaaS providers. This is the category most relevant to migration towards local AI.

**Business users of connected products:**
Companies using connected products that want to access data generated by those products.

**Government authorities:**
In exceptional circumstances, they may request data from companies.

---

## Part 3 — Right of access to data (Art. 4-6)

### The literal text (Art. 4(1))

> "Manufacturers of connected products shall ensure that users can easily, securely and, where relevant, directly access the data generated by the connected product."

And (Art. 5(1)):

> "At the request of a user, the data holder shall make the data available to a third party without undue delay, free of charge, in the requested format and, where technically feasible, continuously and in real time."

### The translation

As a user of a connected product or service, you have the right to the data your use generates. Free of charge. In a usable format. Without undue delay.

This also applies to companies using SaaS services: the data you generate within a cloud application belongs to you. You can request it and transfer it to another service.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

This article is the legal basis for data export from cloud AI services. If you have stored years of conversations, documents and analyses on a cloud AI platform, you have the right to request that data and take it with you.

In practice: before migrating to local AI, you can invoke the Data Act to export your historical data.

---

## Part 4 — Prohibition on switching obstacles (Art. 23-31)

This is the most directly relevant article for organisations wishing to migrate to local AI.

### The literal text (Art. 25(1))

> "Providers of data processing services shall take all reasonable measures to facilitate the switching of customers to a different provider of data processing services."

And (Art. 25(2)):

> "Providers of data processing services shall not impose on customers any commercial, technical, contractual or organisational obstacles that prevent or discourage switching."

### The literal text (Art. 29 — abolition of switching fees)

> "Providers of data processing services shall not charge customers any switching fees in connection with the switching process."

With a transitional period: this prohibition applies in full from **12 January 2027**.

### The translation

Cloud providers are not allowed to make it difficult for you to leave. No contractual lock-in prohibiting migration. No technical barriers making export impossible. And from January 2027: no charges for the switching process.

In practice today: some providers charge considerable "egress fees" for downloading your own data. That will soon be prohibited.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

This is the legal backing for migration to local AI.

When an organisation decides to migrate from cloud AI to local deployment, it has the legal right to:
- Export all data in a usable format
- Do so free of charge (from January 2027)
- Expect the provider to cooperate technically with the migration

The Data Act makes the exit from the cloud not just possible but legally enforceable.

---

## Part 5 — Functional equivalence (Art. 26)

### The literal text

> "Providers of data processing services shall endeavour to achieve functional equivalence after switching, by ensuring that the destination service offers at least the functional level that the source service offered."

### The translation

A cloud provider must actively cooperate to ensure you have the same functionality after the switch. They may not sabotage the migration by not supporting certain functions or supplying data in an unusable format.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

This article protects organisations migrating from cloud AI to local AI against "vendor lock-in by design." If a cloud provider exports its data in a format that your new local system cannot read, that is a violation of the Data Act.

---

## Part 6 — Government access in emergencies (Art. 14-22)

### The literal text (Art. 14(1))

> "A public sector body or a Union institution, body or office may request a data holder to make available the data necessary to respond to a public emergency or to fulfil an exceptional need."

### The translation

In exceptional circumstances — a pandemic, a natural disaster, a terrorist attack — government authorities can request data from companies that hold that data.

This is not a permanent surveillance power. The requirements are strict: there must be a genuine emergency, the requested data must be necessary, and the request must be proportionate.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

This article affects both cloud and local deployments. The difference: with cloud AI, the government has a third party as intermediary. With local AI, the government communicates directly with the organisation.

For most SMEs, this article is less relevant — emergencies where the government needs their data are rare. But for larger organisations in critical sectors it is a familiar provision.

---

## Part 7 — Prohibition on unlawful international data transfers (Art. 32)

### The literal text

> "Providers of data processing services shall take all reasonable technical, legal and organisational measures, including contractual arrangements, to prevent the transfer to or access by a third-country government of non-personal data held in the Union."

### The translation

Cloud providers may not simply transfer your non-personal business data to foreign governments. They must put up legal resistance when a foreign government requests access.

This is a direct response to the CLOUD Act debate: even for non-personal data, Europe wants to ensure that European data cannot simply be requested by foreign governments.

### The implication for local AI versus cloud AI

With local AI, data is at your premises. No cloud provider deciding on your behalf whether to comply with an American court order. You decide. And if a foreign government wants access to your data, that goes through the normal legal channels of your country — not through the internal procedures of an American tech company.

---

## Part 8 — The Data Act in the context of the library

The Data Act is the bridge between what already existed (GDPR, NIS2, DORA) and what is coming (AI Act).

**Towards the past:**
The Data Act strengthens GDPR by also protecting non-personal data and legally enforcing data portability.

**Towards the future:**
The Data Act makes migration from cloud to local AI legally and practically feasible. It removes the exit barrier that cloud providers used as a strategic advantage for years.

**The combination with the AI Act:**
The AI Act obliges organisations to exercise better control over their data and systems. The Data Act gives them the legal instruments to actually exercise that control — by retrieving data from cloud environments and managing it locally.

Together they form a policy package pointing a clear direction: data and intelligence belong with the organisations and people who generate them.

## Glossary

**Connected product** — a product that generates data and is connected to the internet or another network. Smart devices, industrial machinery, vehicles, medical equipment.

**Data holder** — the party that actually has access to data and can share it. Often the manufacturer or service provider.

**Data recipient** — the party to whom data is transferred at the request of the user.

**Provider of data processing services** — cloud providers, data centres, SaaS providers. Any party that processes data on behalf of a customer.

**Switching process** — the totality of technical and organisational actions required to move from one data processing service to another, including data export and import.

**Egress fees** — charges that cloud providers levy for downloading or exporting data. Prohibited from 12 January 2027.

**Functional equivalence** — the principle that a destination service after migration offers at least the same functional level as the source service. Cloud providers must actively contribute to this.

**Non-personal data** — data that cannot directly or indirectly be traced back to an identifiable person. Machine data, production data, sensor data. Falls under the Data Act but not GDPR.
